116. {Partitive genitive.}

The partitive genitive denotes either the whole, a part of which is spoken of, as [ardha nagarasya] (half of the town), [yajJasyAvayavaH] (a part of the sacrifice), Kâd. I , p. 21 [ambaratalasya madhyam] (the middle of the sky) or it carries the notion of selecting out of a multitude as Nir. 1, 12 [vaiyAkaraNAnAmeke] „some of — , among the grammarians”. In the latter case, the genitive is interchangeable with the locative: [manuSyANAM] (or [manuSyeSu])

Examples: of genitive Ait. Br. 1, 5, 25 [zreSThaH svAnAm] (the foremost of his kin), Kathâs. 29, 69 [dhuryo dhanavatAm] (the foremost among the wealthy), Panc. III, 222 [sa ekaiko ‘tra sarvaSAM nItizAstrArthatattvavit]; — of locative Kathâs. 24, 47 [dRSTA putrI yuSmAsu kenacit]; M. 5, 18 [zvAvidhaM zalyakaM godhAM khaGgakUrmazazAMstathA / bhakSyAnpaJcanakheSvAhuranuSTrAMzcaikatodataH]

From the examples given it will be plain, that in Sanskrit, as elsewhere, the partitive cases may not only attend substantives, but all kind of nouns and pronouns.

Rem. 1. If there be meant a staking out of,” the ablative is to be used, cp. 95 , 2°. — R. 1, 2, 15 [krauJcamithunAdekamavadhIH] (you have killed one out of the couple of plovers), cp. Kathâs. 13, 144; 24, 176; Prabodh. V, p. 102 [agneH zeSamRNAccheSaM zatroH zeSaM na zeSayet] (one should not leave a remnant of fire, of a debt, of a foe). (*1)

Rem. 2. It is very common, especially in simple prose, to periphrase the partitive cases by [-madhye] (=gen. or loc.) and [-madhyAt] (= abl.). See 191.

Rem. 3. The partitive construction is unfit to be employed, if the conception of a part selected out of a whole be wanting.

»All of them” = [te sarve], both of us” AvAmubhau (*2).

(*1)

This is the very ablative, enjoined by P. 2, 3, 42. Kâç. is wrong interpreting the sûtra otherwise; Patanjali’s view (I, p. 459) is correct.

(*2)

Yet Mhbh. 1, 37, 8 I have found [sarve naH] = »all of us,” just as in English.
